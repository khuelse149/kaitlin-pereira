About Me

I'm a PhD candidate in Civil & Environmental Engineering at Colorado School of Mines and a 2025–2026 NOAA WINGS Fellow. My research focuses on improving how large lakes are represented in operational weather forecast models.

Using WAVEWATCH III numerical wave modeling, NOAA buoy observations, and NASA SWOT satellite data, I study how wind, waves, and surface roughness interact across the Great Lakes — and how to represent those processes more accurately in forecast models that protect coastal communities during severe winter weather.

I grew up in Idaho with a deep fascination for water and the natural world. That curiosity drives my work today: pushing science forward to deliver more accurate and reliable weather forecasts.

What I Study

01

Winter Wave Model Assessment

Testing WAVEWATCH III accuracy across all five Great Lakes using new winter buoy deployments. Identified that physics parameterization — not wind forcing — drives the 20% wave height bias.

02

SWOT Satellite Integration

Building the first satellite-based spatial validation of wave forecasts in the Great Lakes using NASA’s SWOT mission. Bridging point-based buoy observations to full-domain model evaluation.

03

Surface Roughness & Wind Gustiness

Discovered that wind gustiness can both amplify and suppress waves depending on wave age — a novel “crossover” effect affecting up to 44% of Great Lakes grid points.
